## Transformation Thesis

AI is not a feature checklist. It is an operating-model change. A good AI architecture explains:

- which workflow changes
- who owns the new workflow
- which decision or action improves
- what data makes the system better than a generic tool
- what happens when AI is wrong or unavailable
- how the business measures value after launch

Reject AI architecture proposals that cannot connect model behaviour to a business process, customer outcome, or operating metric.

## 80/20 People-Technology Rule

Most AI transformation effort is people, process, governance, and adoption. Treat technology as the visible 20 percent.

| Workstream | Questions |
|---|---|
| People | Who must trust, review, operate, and improve the AI workflow? |
| Process | What steps are removed, redesigned, or standardised before automation? |
| Data | What source is authoritative, fresh, permitted, and auditable? |
| Controls | What approvals, logs, redactions, budgets, and kill switches exist? |
| Technology | Which model, tool, workflow, or agent architecture fits the proven need? |

## Modernisation Roadmap

1. Map the workflow and baseline cost/time/error/service metrics.
2. Remove waste and clarify ownership before introducing AI.
3. Start with augmentation or draft-first AI where trust is still forming.
4. Add retrieval, tools, or workflow orchestration only when the process needs them.
5. Use agentic autonomy only after evaluation, observability, and governance are stable.
6. Review adoption and business results quarterly.

## Human-Centred AI Design

AI should bridge capability gaps rather than create opaque barriers. Design for:

- explainable recommendations
- user override and correction
- escalation to people
- inclusive language and access
- training and confidence building
- visible limits and uncertainty

Where customer trust matters, the AI experience should make the human service feel more responsive, not absent.

## Governance And Security Spine

Combine governance and security into one spine:

- data classification and access boundary
- model/provider approval
- prompt and tool registry
- input and output guardrails
- tenant and user authorization
- audit logs and retention
- incident response and rollback
- policy review cadence

Governance should be operational enough that a team can answer: who changed what, which model ran, which data was used, what action happened, who approved it, and how to stop it.

## Sustainability And Vendor-Risk Checks

For material AI workloads, include:

- model and infrastructure cost per successful task
- energy/carbon consideration where scale is significant
- vendor lock-in and data portability
- fallback model or manual path
- decommission plan for failed pilots

An AI system that cannot be afforded, audited, or exited is not a sustainable architecture.
