# Experience Mapping Anti-Patterns

Patterns that look like discovery but produce no decisions. Each anti-pattern lists the symptom, the root cause, and the corrective move. Most of these failures come from confusing the *artefact* with the *alignment* it is supposed to produce.

## 1. Wallpaper Map

- **Symptom**: a beautiful, exhaustive impact map or journey map covers an office wall and is referenced once a quarter.
- **Cause**: the map was treated as a deliverable, not a decision tool. The team finished *illustrate* but skipped *align*.
- **Correction**: cut the map until it fits on one page at workshop distance; the next bet must be visible without zooming. A map is "done" only after teams have used it to decide something.

## 2. Persona Theatre

- **Symptom**: personas have stock photos, hobbies, and pet names but no behavioural baselines.
- **Cause**: personas were authored by marketing for narrative, not by research for behaviour.
- **Correction**: replace persona archetypes with named roles plus a recent observed behaviour (the participant's last attempt at the relevant task), the workaround they used, and the cost they paid.

## 3. Future-Wish Validation

- **Symptom**: "9 of 10 interviewees said they would use the feature."
- **Cause**: stated signal counted as behavioural; future-tense answers about hypotheticals treated as evidence.
- **Correction**: discount stated willingness; rebuild the threshold using only behavioural signal — what they did the last time, what they spent, what they have already built around the gap.

## 4. Shopping-List Impact Map

- **Symptom**: dozens of deliverables hang directly off the goal or off generic actors like "users".
- **Cause**: the team jumped over the *How* (impact) layer and connected scope to the goal as if it were self-evidently valuable.
- **Correction**: rebuild the layer the team skipped. Force every deliverable to attach to a specific impact on a specific actor before it is allowed back on the map.

## 5. Generic-User Map

- **Symptom**: actors are listed as "users", "customers", or "stakeholders" without role distinction.
- **Cause**: treating the audience as homogeneous because segmentation is harder than mapping.
- **Correction**: split into specific named actors using Cockburn's primary / secondary / off-stage layering. If two actors are not differentiable on the map, draw two maps.

## 6. Goal Without Metric

- **Symptom**: the central goal reads "improve customer experience" or "increase engagement".
- **Cause**: SMART discipline skipped at session one because measurement felt like a downstream concern.
- **Correction**: pause the workshop until the goal carries a baseline, target, deadline, and measurement method. A weightless goal cannot tell the team whether it has succeeded.

## 7. Map Without Obstacles

- **Symptom**: every actor and impact pushes the goal forward; nothing on the map could prevent the goal.
- **Cause**: optimism bias in stakeholder workshops; people prefer to discuss what to build over what could block them.
- **Correction**: for each important actor, force at least one *negative* or obstructive impact — what they could do to prevent the goal — and brainstorm deliverables that defuse those impacts.

## 8. The Mid-Project Surprise Stakeholder

- **Symptom**: a regulator, partner, or executive emerges mid-build and either changes scope or stops it.
- **Cause**: off-stage actors were not mapped because they do not transact with the product.
- **Correction**: at session one, ask: *who can stop this work even though they will never use the product?* Add them as off-stage actors with their own impacts (often "do not block").

## 9. Long Script, Shallow Probe

- **Symptom**: the interviewer gets through twelve scripted questions in thirty minutes and surfaces little new information.
- **Cause**: the interviewer became a script-runner instead of a listener.
- **Correction**: cut the scripted set to a handful of reusable patterns (procedural, recent-event, magic-wand, workaround, generic-parts). Use the saved time to probe verbatim into specific recent episodes.

## 10. Polite Validation

- **Symptom**: every participant nods, says encouraging things, and the team comes home convinced.
- **Cause**: the interviewer asked questions the participant could answer with stated approval; recruited from convenience pools or friends.
- **Correction**: recruit cold from the segment under hypothesis; pay; ask only about past behaviour; sit through the sixty-second silence. If no participant has tried to solve the problem already at some real cost, the threshold is not met.

## 11. Sample-Padding Until Validation

- **Symptom**: "we're going to do a few more interviews to confirm".
- **Cause**: the team has hit the ten-interview pattern milestone but the pattern is disconfirming, and they hope a larger sample will rescue the hypothesis.
- **Correction**: stop. The hypothesis is mis-framed. Pivot the framing — different actor, different trigger, different workaround — before recruiting more participants.

## 12. Map Owned by No One

- **Symptom**: the map cannot be located, has multiple conflicting versions, or carries data from last year.
- **Cause**: no one owns updates; the map is treated as a one-off output.
- **Correction**: name an owner; set a review cadence (matched to the impact-map review cadence); update the map with new behavioural signal as the source of truth, not stakeholder requests.

## 13. Detail Before Direction

- **Symptom**: the team spends two days on one branch of the map, refining wording on a single touchpoint.
- **Cause**: depth-first construction before breadth has been established.
- **Correction**: build breadth-first. Get all actors and candidate impacts on the map before refining any branch. Most of the depth is wasted because the surrounding scope changes.

## 14. Validation Without Falsification

- **Symptom**: every interview "confirms" the hypothesis.
- **Cause**: the hypothesis is unfalsifiable; the interviewer subconsciously steers toward confirming language.
- **Correction**: write the *one observation* that would force you to abandon the hypothesis. If you cannot, the hypothesis is too vague — sharpen it before continuing.

## 15. Threshold Drift

- **Symptom**: "we said 5/8 but really 3/8 is enough because the qualitative signal is so strong".
- **Cause**: thresholds set after fieldwork rather than before; emotional investment in the hypothesis.
- **Correction**: log the original threshold and the deviation. Move ahead only if the team explicitly downgrades the decision (e.g., "directional, not validated"). Track these deviations across cycles — a team that consistently moves goalposts has a discipline problem, not a research problem.

## 16. Confused Tool Choice

- **Symptom**: a journey map carries multi-actor swimlanes, backstage processes, and support-system rows; or an impact map is being used to track features.
- **Cause**: the team picked the wrong tool for the question and overloaded it.
- **Correction**: separate the artefacts. Multi-actor work belongs in a service blueprint or ecosystem map. Single-actor experience belongs in a journey map. Goal/behaviour decisions belong in an impact map. Each tool answers a different question; mixing them dilutes all three.

## Source Grounding

- Kalbach's four-phase process (initiate, investigate, illustrate, align) and the failure mode of stopping at illustrate.
- Stated-vs-behavioural signal distinction (Alvarez) underpinning Future-Wish Validation and Polite Validation.
- Adzic's anti-patterns: skipping levels (How), maps without obstructive impacts, vague goals without metrics, generic actors.
- Cockburn's primary/secondary/off-stage actors, used to surface mid-project surprise stakeholders.
- The 5/10/15-20 interview cadence and the prescription to pivot framing rather than expand sample.
- The interviewer-as-instrument principle from Lean Customer Development (short scripts, the deliberate sixty-second pause).
