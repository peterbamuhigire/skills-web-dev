# CX/EX Alignment

Customer experience is delivered by employees. When employee experience (EX) is broken, customer experience (CX) cannot recover, no matter how much the front-end is polished. Kalbach makes this explicit: as CX becomes more imperative for business growth, EX becomes equally critical, and the two are *inextricably linked*. Mapping heightens alignment along both dimensions. EX is not a perk programme or a culture statement — it is the lived sum of an employee's interactions with the organisation, including the tools, authority, information, and incentives they bring to each customer moment. This reference encodes the audits and patterns that align them.

## Why EX Is a CX Variable

Frontstage staff translate the service promise into reality at the line of interaction. If their tools, authority, information, time, and incentives are misaligned with that promise, every customer transaction carries silent friction. The customer sees the friction as a CX defect; the cause is upstream in the EX layer. The blueprint is the place to surface and fix it, because the blueprint is the only artefact that holds the customer view and the staff view in the same frame.

Inside-out alignment alone (build a great place to work) does not produce CX value automatically; outside-in alignment alone (polish the front-end) does not survive contact with operations. Kalbach calls the working pattern *inside-across* alignment: align EX to the experience the organisation wants to deliver, then align across the value chain.

## The Four-Dimension EX Audit

For each frontstage role in the blueprint, audit:

1. **Tools** — Can the role complete the customer-facing task in one tool, or do they swivel between systems? How many login screens to start a shift? How many copy-pastes per case?
2. **Authority** — What can the role decide without escalation? What is the cap? Are the caps known to the customer? Have caps been refreshed against current case mix?
3. **Information** — Does the role see what the customer expects them to see (history, prior contacts, promises made)? Is the data current, complete, and trustworthy?
4. **Incentive** — What is the role measured on? Are the measures aligned with the customer outcome the blueprint promises, or with activity counts that distort behaviour?

A failure on any axis becomes a CX failure within weeks. A failure on two becomes a chronic CX failure that no service-recovery programme can patch.

## The EX Journey Map

Just as customers have journeys, employees have them — recruiting, hiring, onboarding, ramp, ongoing work, role change, exit. Map the EX journey for each frontstage role at the same granularity as the CX journey it supports. The phases are real and the moments of truth are real:

- **Recruiting and hiring** — does the candidate's experience match the EX promise made later? Mismatch here pre-loads disengagement.
- **Onboarding and ramp** — how long until the role can deliver the customer experience the blueprint requires? What does the customer see during ramp?
- **Ongoing work** — daily friction (tools, info, authority); the dominant variable in the four-dimension audit.
- **Growth and role change** — does the organisation move people through roles in ways that improve CX, or does it move them away from customer contact as a reward?
- **Exit** — exit interviews carry the strongest EX signal because the cost of honesty has dropped to zero. They are also the most-skipped data source.

A bad EX journey produces silent attrition on the side of the role most exposed to customers. The CX consequences arrive a quarter later as drop in NPS or rise in escalations.

## Aligning the Two Maps

Hold the CX map and the EX map for the same service side by side. The alignment moves:

- **At each customer moment of truth**, identify the supporting employee moment. If the employee moment is poorly designed (no tool, no authority, no information, no incentive), the CX moment will fail.
- **At each EX pain point**, trace forward to the affected customer moments. If the EX pain point has no CX consequence, deprioritise it relative to ones that do; the EX investments that matter most are the ones the customer feels.
- **Compare cycle times**. If the CX timeline expects a response in minutes but the EX support journey resolves in days, the front-line cannot keep the promise. Either fix the support cycle time or change the customer promise.

## Inside-Out, Outside-In, Inside-Across

Use all three views deliberately:

- **Outside-in**: customer perspective drives requirements. The blueprint and journey maps lead.
- **Inside-out**: employee perspective drives capability. The EX journey leads.
- **Inside-across**: align EX with the experience the organisation has promised, then propagate horizontally across functions. The dual-map workshop is the working forum for inside-across alignment.

Skipping inside-across is the dominant cause of services that test well in pilot and fail in operations.

## Co-Creation Across the Two Maps

Run the dual-map workshop with frontstage staff in the room as participants, not subjects. Practical moves:

- Have each frontstage role walk their own day before the customer journey is presented; the blueprint is amended on the spot if the EX reality contradicts the assumed flow.
- Each customer moment of truth is paired with the corresponding employee moment by sticky note. Gaps are made visible.
- Authority caps and incentive metrics are written on cards and laid against the moments of truth. Misaligned cards are the work backlog.

## Indicators of EX/CX Drift

Look for these signs in current operations that the two are diverging:

- Frontstage staff use unofficial workarounds (personal scripts, shared spreadsheets, personal mobile devices) to deliver the promise. The workaround is paying the EX shortfall.
- Customer complaints concentrate at the same handoff month after month while staff training rises without effect. The fix is structural, not knowledge.
- Promotion paths move strong staff away from customer contact. The role that interacts with customers becomes the role no one wants to stay in.
- Internal metrics improve while customer metrics stagnate. The incentive system is rewarding the wrong behaviour.

## When Alignment Is "Done for Now"

- Each frontstage role has a four-dimension audit on file with date.
- An EX journey is drawn for each frontstage role at the same phase granularity as the CX journey.
- CX moments of truth are paired with the corresponding EX moments and gaps documented.
- Authority caps and incentive metrics have been reviewed against the current case mix in the last twelve months.
- A dual-map review is on the calendar at a fixed cadence.

## Source Grounding

- Kalbach's framing of EX as "how employees live the company culture over time" and as a CX variable, not a separate concern.
- The "inextricably linked" framing of CX and EX requiring inside-across alignment.
- The end-to-end employee journey covering recruiting, hiring, onboarding, ramp, ongoing work, role change, and exit.
- Pairing customer moments of truth with corresponding employee moments using a dual-map workshop.
- Inside-out, outside-in, and inside-across as three deliberate alignment views.
- Frontstage authority and information dependencies inherited from Stickdorn et al.'s frontstage/backstage model.
