# Buyer Proof Curation

A premium offering does not get bought on a logo wall. It gets bought when the buyer can rebuild the case for it in their own words, to a sceptical colleague, the day after they last saw the seller. Proof is the material that makes that re-construction possible. Generic proof, by contrast, is treated by careful buyers as a low-credibility cue and can actively reduce persuasion when the buyer is paying attention.

The persuasion research is direct: under high elaboration, attitudes formed through carefully scrutinised arguments are more persistent, more resistant to counter-attack, and more predictive of behaviour than attitudes formed through peripheral cues. Premium buyers elaborate. So proof must be designed to survive scrutiny, not to impress at a glance.

## The four jobs of proof

Curated proof does four jobs. Material that does not do at least one of them does not belong in the bundle:

1. **Reduce perceived risk** for the specific objection the buyer has surfaced.
2. **Anchor the comparator** so the buyer is comparing your offering to the right alternative.
3. **Illustrate the change in behaviour** the offering produced - not features, not awards.
4. **Authorise the buyer** to recommend you internally without having to defend their judgment alone.

If a piece of proof checks none of those boxes, it is decoration. Decoration is suspicion-inducing under scrutiny.

## Curation: matching proof to risk

Generic case-study walls fail premium buyers because they are not matched. Curate by reading the buyer's risk profile from discovery and selecting:

- **The closest analogue.** Same industry, same buyer role, same scale of decision. One precise analogue beats five distant ones.
- **The most adverse precedent.** A case where the customer started skeptical, or the deployment hit a known problem, and the offering still delivered. Premium buyers trust evidence that survived contact with reality more than evidence from easy wins.
- **The exit story.** A reference customer who has been with you long enough that the renewal-or-leave decision has been made at least twice. Survival across renewals is harder to fake than initial enthusiasm.
- **The proximate authority.** A peer of the buyer (same title, same kind of organisation) who is willing to take a phone call. Distant celebrity logos are a peripheral cue; a peer reference is a central-route argument.

Decision rule: if a buyer has named three specific concerns, a curated proof bundle has at most three pieces - one per concern - plus a peer reference. More material does not increase persuasion under scrutiny; under high elaboration, weak arguments hurt.

## Testimonial capture

The strongest testimonial is the one written in the customer's voice, about a measurable change, with permission to use it. Capture them on purpose, not by accident.

The capture script that works:
1. After the customer has stated the value verbally: "The reason you like this is because it [increases / reduces / improves] [specific outcome]. Is that right?"
2. "I'd love to have more satisfied clients like you. If it's okay with you, I'll jot down what you've shared with me and send it to you."
3. "If what I've written meets with your approval, please ask your assistant to type it on your company's letterhead and send it back to me."
4. "Would you help me build my client base by letting others know how I've helped you reach your goals?"

Why this script works: the customer agrees to the substance before being asked to put pen to paper, and the seller does the writing. The asymmetry that traps most testimonial requests - "could you write something nice when you have time?" - never occurs.

For higher-stakes references, ask before the engagement starts: "If we deliver what we are promising, would you be willing to take a peer call once a quarter?" Capturing the willingness while the buyer is energised, not after the work has cooled, materially changes the conversion to actual reference availability.

## Word of mouth as a deliberate output

Marketing case studies show word-of-mouth as a category-redefining channel rather than a passive byproduct - Walkers' Nobby's Nuts launched without parent-brand endorsement and was fast-tracked to nationwide fame primarily through word-of-mouth, achieving the awareness and trial a new product needs. The lesson for premium sellers: design the engagement so that talking about it becomes natural for the buyer. That requires:

- A specific, quotable result the buyer can repeat ("we cut [process] from 6 weeks to 4 days") instead of a vague satisfaction.
- A surprising-enough story that the buyer wants to be the one telling it (a positive surprise that exceeded expectations).
- A small ritual at the end of the engagement that crystallises the story (a written summary in the buyer's voice, a signed result memo, a recorded short interview).

Anti-pattern: relying on the survey score as the trigger for word-of-mouth. Survey numbers do not travel.

## Proof under skeptical conditions

Persuasion research finds that messages from less-trusted sources receive more careful scrutiny than messages from trusted sources. The implication for proof: in front of a sceptical buyer, weak proof is worse than no proof. A buyer who finds one inflated number on slide three reads every subsequent claim with a counter-argument loaded.

Practical rules under scepticism:
- **Cite the unflattering number.** A case study that includes the cost of switching, the months until ROI, the team turbulence during deployment - and still lands - is far more persuasive than one that reports only upside.
- **Source every quantitative claim** to a specific document, named contact, or published filing. "Independent measurement" without naming who did the measuring is a peripheral cue, not an argument.
- **Do not mix categories of evidence.** Do not list a market-research stat, an internal benchmark, a customer quote, and an analyst rating on the same slide as if they were equivalent. They differ in epistemic weight, and a careful buyer notices.

## Trust signals that survive scrutiny versus those that do not

Survives:
- Named reference willing to take a peer call.
- Customer-written quote on the customer's letterhead, with role and date.
- Public filing or audited number.
- Independent third-party review with author, date, and method declared.
- A sample of work done for the buyer's domain that the buyer can verify in five minutes.

Does not survive:
- "Industry-leading" without a referent.
- "Trusted by Fortune 500" with logos but no named users.
- Awards from organisations that exist primarily to sell awards.
- Unattributed quotes ("a CEO at a major bank said...").
- Round-number percentage improvements with no baseline.

## Decision rules

- One precise analogue beats five distant ones.
- A peer who will take a phone call beats a celebrity logo.
- A case study that names what went wrong is more persuasive than one that did not.
- If the buyer has named three concerns, ship three matched pieces of proof - not ten.
- Testimonial capture is a script, run on purpose, immediately after value has been stated.
- Proof that fails to do one of the four jobs (reduce risk, anchor comparator, illustrate change, authorise the buyer) is decoration and is removed.

## Source Grounding

- The persuasion-research finding that under high elaboration, careful scrutiny rewards strong arguments and punishes weak ones - the basis for "less, sharper proof" rather than logo walls.
- The "I'll Write What You Said" script for capturing customer testimonials in the customer's words and on the customer's letterhead.
- The Walkers / Nobby's Nuts case study from Marketing Excellence: a launch designed for word-of-mouth, achieving high awareness and trial without parent-brand endorsement.
- The Tesco loyalty-segment commitment-profile model, in which evidence is matched to where the customer is in the relationship rather than presented uniformly.
- The Priester and Petty source-trust finding that less-trusted sources trigger more scrutiny - the basis for the rule that weak proof under scepticism is worse than no proof.
- The Toyota measurement-culture practice of tying every campaign metric back to a small set of board-visible KPIs (test drives, brochure requests) - applied here as the discipline of citing only proof tied to a verifiable outcome.
