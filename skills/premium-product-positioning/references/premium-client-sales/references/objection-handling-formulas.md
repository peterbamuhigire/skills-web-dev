# Objection Handling Formulas

Buyers raise objections when they are unconvinced of their need or when they want to express an opinion different from yours. Inside every objection are clues to their real concerns, and behind those concerns are the dominant desires and fears that drive the decision - their hot buttons. The objection is data, not an attack.

The amount of success in selling correlates directly with the ability to handle and overcome blocks. Easy sales - those that need no convincing - are rare and unprofitable. Sales careers are built on the deals that required a third response.

## The first principle: never disagree, never agree, only understand

Disagreeing antagonises. Agreeing convinces the buyer their position is correct. Neither moves the deal. The job at the moment of objection is to understand - hear the whole story, never interrupt, then surface the buying interest.

Standard openers when an objection lands:
- "I'm sure you have a good reason for saying that. Please share it with me."
- "What makes you say that? What is the reason?"
- "I see you feel quite strongly about this, so there must be a reason for your feeling as you do. I'd appreciate it if you'd share that reason with me."

Always isolate before responding: "Is it only a matter of [the stated objection]?" / "Is that the only thing holding you back?" The answer separates a real obstacle from a polite no.

## Formula 1: Price is too high

Pattern: do not defend the number. Reframe to value, then anchor.

Sequence:
1. **Affirm** the concern is legitimate: "Price is an important consideration, isn't it? Would you consider value equally important?"
2. **Ask for the comparator**: "What are you comparing our offering to?" / "Compared to what?" / "What price were you planning to pay?" Without a comparator the objection is a feeling, not a calculation.
3. **Convert price to cost-over-life**: "How many years do you expect to use this? Over [N] years that comes to [$X] per year, which is [$X] per day for the finest [product] in the industry. That's a fair offer, isn't it?" The principle: minimise the price by reducing it to its smallest component; magnify the cost of inferior alternatives over the same period.
4. **Distinguish price from cost**: "You pay the price only once - when you purchase. Cost concerns you for as long as you own it. You might find a lower price out there. You won't find a lower cost."
5. **Trial-close on resolution**: "If we can resolve the cost to your satisfaction, would you want to purchase today?" / "If you can convince yourself that the price is more than fair, would you be in a position to proceed with a yes decision today?"

Variants:
- "Price concerns you today. Quality concerns you every day."
- "We're not cheap. We're good. I'd hate to think we offered you [a product] with [N]% more than anyone else can offer and didn't charge more for it."
- "The value of this is what it will do for you, not what you have to pay for it."
- "If you think this costs too much, how will you pay for the problems, headaches, and expenses that result from not using it?"

Anti-pattern: agreeing to take options out of the package as the first response. Ask "what would you like to eliminate?" only to surface what they actually do not value, not to start a discount negotiation.

## Formula 2: I need to check out your competitors

The objection is healthy. Educated buyers do compare. Treat the comparison as the path forward, not an obstacle.

Sequence:
1. **Welcome the comparison**: "That's good. What criteria will you be using? Please share which competitors you're considering - I can help you compare features and benefits."
2. **Borrow the homework**: "Many of our customers used that strategy. In many ways they did your homework for you. Let's go over what they found and why they elected to go with us."
3. **Ask them to score you now**: "Based on what you know about us, would you share what you believe are our strong points and our weaknesses?" - this surfaces the gaps before the competitor does.
4. **Set the comparison frame**: "Are we comparing apples and oranges? Our firm is the [diamond] of our industry - please don't compare us to a [cubic zirconia]." Use only when the comparison is genuinely category-mismatched.

If the buyer wants to research alone, propose a structured next step: "I compare options daily for clients. I'll gladly answer any questions you pose - that will save you time and effort."

## Formula 3: I'm not interested / send me literature

This is most often a defensive reflex, not a final decision. The response is curiosity, not pressure.

Sequence:
1. **Reframe the reflex**: "Many of our loyal customers began our relationship with just those words. It wasn't until I demonstrated how this could help their bottom line that they became really interested."
2. **Force a binary**: "Whenever someone asks me to send literature instead of meeting, I find one of two things is true: either the person is very interested and wants as much information as possible, or they're not at all interested and the literature is an easy way to say no. So I don't waste your time, would you mind telling me which applies?"
3. **If genuinely not interested, ask why**: "I'm sure you have a good reason for feeling this isn't of interest. Would you share it with me?"
4. **Reduce the time ask**: "Literature frequently raises more questions than it answers. In just fifteen minutes I can demonstrate how this benefits you. Is [day] at [time] good, or [other day] at [other time]?"

## Formula 4: We want to think it over

The stall is rarely about thinking. It is about an unsurfaced concern or a missing decision-maker. The job is to surface, not to push.

Sequence:
1. **Welcome it cheerfully**: "Great. What do we need to think about?" The simplest version often gets the best answer.
2. **Step out so they can talk**: offer to make a phone call, leave the room, or pause - then return to answer questions.
3. **Probe for the real issue**: "Would you share with me what is really holding you back from making this decision?" / "What is the main concern you have left?"
4. **Picture-shift**: walk them four to six months forward - the CEO is praising them for the action that fixed [the problem they opened with]. Whether they appear in that picture depends on the action they take now.
5. **Offer the four-question close**: do you like it; do you want to use it; can you afford it; when would you like to start enjoying the benefits? "Now, is there anything else you need to think about?"

Anti-pattern: giving them three weeks of silence. Set a specific next checkpoint with two time alternatives.

## Formula 5: We are going to wait until [next quarter / a future trigger]

Sequence:
1. **Assumptive close**: "Then your order is firm. Great - let's fill out the paperwork and ship on the first day of the next quarter."
2. **Concession close**: "Why don't we ship today and delay the billing until [trigger date]? You can start getting the benefit right away."
3. **Cost of waiting**: "What do you hope to gain by waiting? What might you lose by waiting?" / "If during the time you're thinking you lose your competitive edge in the marketplace, that could be very costly."
4. **Paperwork-now close**: "Your plan is to purchase next quarter? Let's fill out the paperwork now, and we'll get started the day you select."

## Formula 6: Times are tough / business is slow

Reframe so that doing nothing costs more than doing something.

- "That makes this the perfect time to talk. This is designed to help companies like yours get through tough times."
- "You can't keep a patient healthy by withholding medicine that's required. The same holds true for a business."
- "Tough times call for you to be able to increase profits. Using this can help you do that."
- Risk inversion: "Why not limit your order instead of not placing one at all? That also limits your risk."

## Formula 7: We must be loyal to our current supplier

Acknowledge loyalty, then redirect it.

- "I respect your sense of loyalty. I think your first loyalty should be to the well-being of your own company, don't you?"
- "If I can show you that this is better for your company than the one you are currently using, you would consider changing - or at least sampling - wouldn't you?"
- "Some of our best customers today used to use our competition yesterday. Let me tell you why they switched."
- "How many of the suppliers you did business with ten years ago do you still do the same amount of business with today?"

## Formula 8: I need to share this with my boss

Sequence:
1. **Take the meeting up the ladder**: "Why don't you just introduce me to [him/her], and let me do the work? That's what I'm paid to do."
2. **Map the decision committee**: "Who else is part of the decision-making process? Is your boss involved in all purchase decisions?"
3. **Schedule the joint conversation**: "Now that you're favourably impressed, can I count on you to accompany me so I can present these benefits to [your boss / others involved]?"

## Formula 9: We've decided not to buy from you

Treat the loss as a learning event, but check first whether it is final.

- "What changed your mind?" / "Where did I go wrong?"
- "Today, or ever? Today I can reluctantly accept. Forever leaves me very disturbed - it was my impression that helping your company prepare for the future was a top priority."
- "It is with your feedback that we can continually improve. What did you prefer about [their offering]? If we were to bid for your business again, where would you suggest we focus improvement efforts?"

## Decision rules across all formulas

- Hear the whole objection. Never interrupt.
- Never disagree. Never agree. Understand.
- Isolate: "Is it only [this]? Is that the only thing holding you back?"
- Use feel / felt / found when honest: "I know how you feel. At times I felt that way myself. Do you know what I found?"
- Trial-close after every successful response, to confirm the objection is actually resolved before moving on.
- If you cannot resolve the objection, do not paper over it - schedule a specific follow-up with a named action.

## Source Grounding

- The "Countering Objections Positively" principle: never agree, never disagree, only understand; objections expose hot buttons (dominant desires or fears).
- The Compared-To / Planning-to-Pay / Daily-Cost / Cost-per-Day battery for converting price into cost-over-life and a per-day figure.
- The "Price or Cost" distinction (price paid once at purchase, cost endured for the life of ownership).
- The Apples-and-Oranges and Their-Specifications scripts for handling competitor comparison without disparagement.
- The Interested-or-Not-Interested binary script for the "send literature" reflex.
- The Let's-Picture and four-question (like / want / afford / start) close for the think-it-over stall.
- The Withholding-Medicine and Market-Share frames for the "times are tough" objection.
