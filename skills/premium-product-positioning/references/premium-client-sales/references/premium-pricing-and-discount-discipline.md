# Premium Pricing and Discount Discipline

A premium price is the indicator of quality. When the price is changed, the value perception of the product changes too. The discipline is to compete on what the offering does for the buyer over the life of ownership, not on what it costs at the moment of purchase.

The case studies of brands that sustained premium positions show a pattern: they treated price as a brand decision, not a sales tactic. Stella Artois held to a premium price as an indicator of quality - the positioning was that product superiority is worth paying more for, with a sophisticated tone of voice that signalled who the drinker was. The Famous Grouse delivered a price premium because over half of survey respondents said it was "a brand worth paying more for". Green & Black's grew by repositioning from "worthy organic" to "luxury premium chocolate", leading on taste with organic as supporting evidence rather than the primary reason to purchase. In a category growing at 0.5%, sales value rose 72% year-on-year over a twelve-week period. The lesson: premium is earned by changing what the brand stands for, not by adding a markup.

## The pillars that earn the right to a premium

A premium price is defensible only when at least three pillars are visibly in place:

1. **A category-redefining positioning.** Green & Black's stopped being an organic brand and became a luxury chocolate brand. Tesco built earned loyalty around Clubcard data and a redefined customer relationship, not around price. The buyer is paying for a different thing, not the same thing at a higher price.
2. **Consistency across every touch point.** Freeview built brand consistency from postcode database to call centre to point-of-sale. A premium that survives in advertising but evaporates in support is not a premium.
3. **Inherent superiority that the buyer can verify.** A year from now, price will not concern the buyer if the offering lives up to expectations. If it does not, even a discount looks expensive.
4. **Tone of voice that signals who the buyer is by buying.** Premium signals identity as much as utility. Stella's positioning ran for years on the discipline of saying, in many ways, that this is what people who are rich and clever drink.
5. **Pricing power proven in the market.** The single best test is whether buyers say, in research, that this is "a brand worth paying more for". If they do not, no list price is safe.

If two or more pillars are missing, the offering is not premium yet, regardless of the list price. Discount pressure on an under-built premium will collapse the position; the right answer is to invest in the pillars before defending the price.

## Premium pricing language for the seller

When the buyer raises price, the seller must not retreat into discount talk. The vocabulary that holds the line:

- "We're not cheap. We're good. I'd hate to think we offered you [a product] with [N]% more than anyone else can offer and didn't charge more for it."
- "High quality products or services are not cheap, and cheap products or services are not of high quality."
- "Our company had a choice. We could either design this to do as little as possible so we could sell it for a pittance, or we could design it to do as much as possible so that over many years your cost would be lower than otherwise."
- "Don't you agree it makes more sense to invest in the best now than to pay for something that is ultimately inferior?"
- "Price concerns you today. Quality concerns you every day."
- "Aren't you better off paying a little more than you expected rather than a lot less than you need to ensure quality?"
- "The value of this is what it will do for you, not what you have to pay for it."

These are not closing tricks. They are the seller's framing of what the buyer is buying. If the seller does not believe them, the buyer will not either.

## The price-versus-cost reframe

Premium pricing reframes from price (paid once at purchase) to cost (endured across ownership). The reframe has three working forms:

- **Daily cost reduction**: take the increment of premium price over the alternative, divide by years of use, divide by 365. "That computes to [$X] a day over the life of the product. [N] years of superior performance is worth [$X] a day, isn't it?"
- **Recovery period**: "Your investment is about [$X] per month. We estimate you'll recoup your investment in about [N] months, and that's not figuring in the extra benefits from improved morale / output / risk-reduction."
- **Cost of inferior alternative**: "If you pay less than you should and what you end up with creates rather than alleviates problems, we're talking trouble. Not being willing to pay for quality could end up being very costly."

Use the reframe before the buyer asks for a discount, not after. Once the buyer has named a number lower than yours, price has become the topic; the reframe gets harder.

## Discount discipline: rules before the conversation

Most discount erosion happens because the seller did not decide in advance what would and would not be traded. Set the rules before the conversation:

1. **Never discount the headline price without removing scope, lengthening commitment, or moving timing.** A unilateral price cut signals that the original price was not real. Every concession on price must be paired with a concession from the buyer.
2. **The first discount is the floor, not the start.** Premium buyers test. If the first discount given is large, they will assume more is available. Make the first concession small and conditional, paired with a request: "If we can get to that price, can you commit by [date] / increase volume to [N] / sign a [N]-year term?"
3. **Trade structure first, price second.** Payment terms, scope reduction, timing, length of commitment, references and case-study rights, exclusive-period waivers - all are concessions that cost less than headline price.
4. **Eliminate-options reframe**: when a buyer pushes price, ask "what options would you like to eliminate from the package?" The answer either reveals what they did not value (legitimate scope reduction) or surfaces that they value everything and were testing.
5. **No retroactive discounts.** Do not retro a deal that has already closed. It rewrites the buyer's memory of every future negotiation.
6. **Walk-away authority sits with the seller in the room.** A seller who must escalate every concession appears low-status, which weakens both pricing power and the buyer's confidence.

## What the buyer is actually asking when they ask for a discount

Treat the discount request as a probe. Three things it might mean:

- "I want to know if your price is real." Hold the line, calmly. The hold itself is data the buyer needs.
- "I do not see enough value." Stop negotiating; go back to discovery. The objection is not price; it is unproven worth.
- "My internal process requires that I show I extracted something." Trade structure, not headline. Give the buyer something to bring back to procurement that does not weaken the brand.

Diagnose before responding. Sellers who answer all three with the same percentage cut leave money on the table on calls one and three and leave deals unsigned on call two.

## Decision rules

- If the buyer cannot articulate the value, do not discount - return to qualifying.
- If the buyer can articulate the value but is constrained, trade structure for price.
- If the buyer is comparing on price alone, the deal is mis-qualified - the buyer is not in the premium category.
- Anti-pattern: matching a competitor's discount to "stay in the deal". Better lost than won at a price that misprices every future deal.
