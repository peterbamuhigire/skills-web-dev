# Sales Conversation Arc

A premium engagement is "sensible selling": the seller controls the buyer rather than visiting with them. Each stage has a decision rule and an exit gate. Skipping stages is the most common cause of prospects who like you but never buy. Asking for the order is the only way to consummate a sale - close the C and you are left with lose, for both parties.

## Stage 1 - Discipline before the call

Before any contact, run the daily-development discipline: track cold calls to new prospects, calls in response to inquiries, calls from referrals, calls to existing accounts, calls to former accounts, and the appointments scheduled from each. As a working assumption, plan on roughly ten prospecting attempts to land one decision-maker meeting; if your ratio is materially worse, you are turning prospects off, not running out of names.

Two behaviours that quietly destroy the arc are procrastination (failing to act in time, usually rooted in fear of rejection, fear of embarrassment, fear of failing to elicit a response, or fear of having to follow through with a close) and disorganisation (not having qualifying questions ready, not tracking prior calls). They feed each other. Conquer one and the other gets easier.

Exit gate: today's prospecting block is scheduled, the call list is in front of you, and the qualifying questions for the call are written down.

## Stage 2 - Initial contact

Goal: earn an in-person or scheduled conversation, not a sale.

Required structure for a cold call:
- Greet by name
- Identify yourself and your company
- State a single high-value reason the prospect should give you minutes (a specific dollar figure they could save, a specific bottom-line increase, a specific risk they could avoid)
- Pause briefly but do not wait for a verbal answer
- Close immediately on a meeting time, offering exactly two alternatives ("[day] at [time], or [other day] at [other time]?")

Variants by source:
- Cold-to-new: lead with the saving or the leadership angle
- Referral call: name the referring source first, then state what you did for them and why they suggested you
- Former-client call: acknowledge they switched, name what has materially changed in your offering, ask for time to update them
- Gatekeeper: take command politely - first and last name, brief reason framed in terms of the prospect's interest, treat the gatekeeper as someone who can help the boss win

Decision rule: always close for the appointment when you can. If you cannot get the appointment, ask for permission to recontact and a date.

Exit gate: a meeting is scheduled with two firm time anchors, or the prospect is reclassified as nurture/dead.

## Stage 3 - Opener and rapport (the first minutes of the meeting)

Goal: shift from your time being valuable to their interest being aroused. If you communicate that your time matters, the prospect believes what you have to say matters.

Effective openers all share one trait: they ask the prospect to take a position before you pitch. Use one:
- "If there were a way I could help you [meet/exceed your goal], you would want to hear about it, wouldn't you?"
- "How important is it to you to increase [profits/morale/productivity]?"
- "I'm not certain whether my ideas apply to your situation. May I ask you one question first?"
- Curiosity opener: "I'd like to share an idea that our clients tell us [makes them money / cuts costs / reduces turnover]."

Anti-pattern: opening with a feature dump or company history. Both move the spotlight off the prospect, and prospects who are not in the spotlight stop listening.

Exit gate: the prospect has answered your opening question with words, not a head nod, and has agreed in principle that the topic matters.

## Stage 4 - Qualifying (controlling the buyer)

Most salespeople fail not from product ignorance but from people ignorance: they avoid the tough questions because the questions might produce a no. Sensible sellers ask anyway. Score yourself on whether you routinely ask, in some form:

- "If I can demonstrate how [our product] improves productivity, quality, or profit, this is something you would want to use - am I right?"
- "How long have you been thinking about [owning / using] this?"
- "Who besides you will be making the decision?"
- "How is the purchase decision made? How soon do you plan to make it?"
- "What will I need to win your approval? To win the approval of others?"
- "If you had carte blanche, describe your ideal [product/service]."
- "What is not [owning/using] this costing you now?"
- "What is keeping you from [owning/using] this now?"

Trial-close embedded here: "If our product meets all of your criteria, when can we [get started / install / deliver]?" Use it to flush out latent objections early, while the cost of discovering them is still low.

Decision rule: prospects pay for value, not for price. If the buyer cannot articulate what they value, you have not finished qualifying.

Exit gate: you can name the decision-maker, the decision process, the decision timeline, the criteria, and at least one specific cost of inaction.

## Stage 5 - Establishing needs and wants

Goal: get the prospect to explain to themselves, in their own words, why they would buy. Buyer-built reasons are durable; seller-supplied reasons evaporate.

Useful question forms:
- "Could you tell me how you evaluated the [product/service] you are using now?"
- "If you could invent a product to solve all of your company's problems in this area, what would it do?"
- "What do you like best about the [product/service] you are using now?" (then: "What would you change?")
- "The major benefits this delivers are [A, B, C]. Which interests you most? Why is that important to you? What would that do for you?"

Build the presentation around the buyer, never give the presentation at them. Effective sales presentations are built around buyers, not delivered to them.

Exit gate: the prospect has stated, unprompted, the specific benefit category that matters most to them.

## Stage 6 - Presentation tied to the buyer's hot button

Now, and only now, present. Each feature is voiced in three parts: the feature, the benefit, and the end-result the buyer can visualise. Repeatedly hit the buyer's hot button (the dominant desire or fear surfaced in Stage 5) - each hit raises saleability.

Decision rule: if you are tempted to add features the buyer did not ask about, stop. Adding scope at this stage trades excitement for confusion.

Exit gate: the prospect has made a buying signal - leans in, asks ownership questions, becomes more animated, asks about logistics. When SQ (their seriousness) goes up, your TCQ (trial-closing) must go up to match.

## Stage 7 - Trial close and objection handling

Use trial closes to take the buyer's temperature without forcing a decision:
- "How long have you been considering [this]?"
- "How do you feel about it?"
- "On a scale of 1 to 10, with 10 being ready to purchase, where are you?"
- "In order to attain your goals, would it be worth an investment of [cost]?"

If the answer reveals an objection, move to objection handling (see objection-handling-formulas.md). The rule is unchanging: never agree, never disagree, only understand. Hear the whole story before responding.

Exit gate: every surfaced objection has been addressed, and the buyer has confirmed there is nothing else holding them back.

## Stage 8 - Close

Closing is the natural end of the arc, not a separate event. Use the simplest close that fits:
- Direct close: "Now that you see the benefits, when would you like delivery / to get started?"
- Pre-commitment close: "We agreed your top criteria were [list]. We have met them. With your approval, we can begin immediately."
- Choice close: present two affirmative options, both of which are sales.
- Win-the-business close: "What do I need to do to win your business?" (sincere, not desperate)

Anti-pattern: a hasty close on a buyer who is not yet wanting enough. Even the best salesperson cannot close a buyer who does not want what is on offer; the trial-close result tells you whether you are there yet.

Exit gate: paperwork is in motion, or the buyer has set a specific next decision date with named participants.

## Stage 9 - After the sale: court the client and seed the next sale

After the first sale, plant the seeds for the next one - do not aim only for a one-time run when you should be planning a series. Three things to schedule before leaving the meeting where the deal closed:

1. A delivery / kickoff confirmation contact within the week.
2. A check-in once the buyer has had time to experience results, scripted to capture a quotable testimonial in their own words ("If it's okay, I'll jot down what you've shared and send it back; if it meets with your approval, please put it on letterhead and send it back to me").
3. A referral request, naming a specific number ("the names of just three of your business acquaintances I could call and say you suggested I meet with"). Sources of referrals are unlimited - they are resting all around you, waiting for a question.

Decision rule: a satisfied client who is not asked for a referral is a wasted asset. Treat asking as part of the job, not as imposing.

Exit gate: a follow-up cadence is on the calendar and at least one referral or testimonial commitment is captured.

## Source Grounding

- Daily Business Development tracking form (cold / inquiry / referral / existing / former call counts to appointments).
- Self-Survey of the four sales fears (rejection, embarrassment, failure to elicit, succeeding and having to follow through).
- Quality Qualifiers list of qualifying questions, including "if I had carte blanche" and "what is not owning/using costing you now".
- Trial-close battery, including the 1-to-10 readiness scale and pre-commitment ("if I can demonstrate today our product will meet all the criteria, do we have a basis for doing business?").
- Sensible-selling principle that effective presentations are built around buyers, not given to them, and the SQ / TCQ pairing for buying signals.
- Post-sale referral scripts ("Names of Three", "I'll Write What You Said", "Promise Me" self-referral).
