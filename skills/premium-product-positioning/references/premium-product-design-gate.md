# Premium Product Design Gate

Use this gate for software, SaaS, apps, websites, dashboards, strategy deliverables, and agency services aimed at high-value buyers.

## 1. Buyer Definition

- Name the economic buyer, technical buyer, user buyer, influencer, gatekeeper, and approver.
- Identify whether the sale is personal, executive, enterprise, committee-led, procurement-led, or lifestyle/status-led.
- Define what the buyer fears: wasted time, embarrassment, compliance failure, poor adoption, data loss, weak ROI, reputational damage, or looking ordinary.
- Define what the buyer wants beyond function: confidence, status, expert guidance, faster progress, access, discretion, certainty, or measurable advantage.

## 2. Offer Architecture

- State the premium promise in one sentence: buyer, outcome, timeframe or proof point, and risk reduction.
- Package the offer around outcomes, not hours or features.
- Use tiers only when each tier has a clear buyer type and decision logic.
- Include onboarding, implementation, review, support, and success reporting as part of the product experience.
- Define exclusions and change-control rules so premium service does not become unlimited labour.

## 3. Product Experience

- First impression: clear visual hierarchy, fast load, excellent copy, no generic stock feel, no clutter.
- First value: the buyer can see a meaningful result, dashboard, workflow, or configured state quickly.
- Trust: security, privacy, reliability, auditability, source evidence, testimonials, case studies, and support paths are visible.
- Control: executives can see progress, performance, risks, approvals, spend, and outcomes without chasing staff.
- Personalisation: the product can reflect the buyer's industry, role, company, brand, workflow, and reporting needs.
- Simplicity: premium products reduce effort before adding features. Navigation, onboarding, forms, dashboards, reports, and support flows must feel obvious without hiding serious buyer proof.

## 3A. Packaging And Perceived Value

- Treat packaging as the first proof of quality: name, onboarding, pricing page, screenshots, proposal, demo, documentation, support experience, and handover assets.
- Align packaging with the price tier. A high-ticket product needs premium copy, visual restraint, proof, risk reduction, and service evidence before the buyer sees the full product.
- Use premium signals honestly: scarcity, customisation, senior access, private review, implementation support, and priority service only when the operation can deliver them.
- Avoid cheap signals: discount-led positioning, generic templates, cluttered UI, vague claims, thin documentation, and sloppy support responses.
- For software and websites, the "package" includes performance, security, SEO, analytics, accessibility, and account ownership, not only what the screen looks like.

## 4. Pricing Power

- Anchor price to value, consequence, risk removed, or executive outcome, not only cost.
- Avoid default discounts. Trade price only for reduced scope, slower timeline, fewer service components, longer commitment, or lower risk to the seller.
- Use fixed-fee packages for defined transformations and retainers for ongoing strategic support.
- Show the cost of inaction where credible: lost revenue, wasted labour, leakage, risk exposure, missed opportunity, or brand damage.
- Make payment terms support delivery quality and cash flow.

## 5. Premium Proof

- Use evidence the buyer respects: executive case studies, before/after metrics, audited process, named methodology, screenshots, demos, references, pilot results, credentials, certifications, or governance artefacts.
- For service and software that is hard to inspect before purchase, make the invisible visible: process map, timeline, sample deliverables, reporting cadence, risk controls, and escalation path.
- Every big claim needs proof or a softer phrasing.
- Build authority assets for high-value buyers: diagnostic, checklist, benchmark, comparison guide, implementation roadmap, case study, calculator, or executive briefing. These assets support SEO, outreach, proposal quality, and sales follow-up.

## 6. Enterprise Selling Support

- Prepare account research before outreach: triggers, initiatives, likely pains, current systems, competitors, budget owner, and decision path.
- Use discovery to uncover situation, problem, implication, and payoff before pitching.
- Ask for concrete next-step commitments: meeting, diagnostic, data access, stakeholder introduction, pilot, proposal review, or procurement path.
- Build the business case for the committee: value, risk, implementation effort, adoption, budget, timeline, and proof.

## Blocking Red Flags

- The product looks expensive but does not save time, reduce risk, improve status, or create measurable value.
- Pricing is premium but onboarding/support/reporting are ordinary.
- The buyer is broad and undefined.
- The pitch relies on "best quality" with no proof.
- The sales process asks for a decision before creating trust and diagnosing the problem.
