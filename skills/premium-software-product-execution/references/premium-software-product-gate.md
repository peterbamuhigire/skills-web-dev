# Premium Software Product Gate

Use this gate for premium SaaS, ERP, POS, dashboards, websites, client portals, agency-built systems, and software proposals.

## 1. Buyer And Fit

- Name the economic buyer, technical buyer, daily users, influencers, blockers, procurement owner, and final approver.
- Define the buyer's expensive pain: lost revenue, wasted labour, leakage, weak controls, slow reporting, poor brand trust, compliance exposure, customer churn, or executive uncertainty.
- State why this buyer deserves premium execution and what proof they need before committing.
- Disqualify sub-premium work: vague outcomes, bargain-seeking buyer, no discovery, no access to decision makers, no budget for quality, or unwillingness to support proper implementation.

## 2. Value And Pricing Logic

- Convert outcomes into numbers where possible: hours saved, error rate reduced, cash leakage prevented, conversion lifted, retention improved, revenue unlocked, or risk avoided.
- Price against value and risk removed, not only hours worked.
- Package deliverables into clear tiers or phases only when each option has a different buyer, risk level, or implementation depth.
- Trade concessions for something concrete: smaller scope, slower timeline, longer commitment, upfront payment, fewer revisions, reduced support, or lower delivery risk.
- Define the cost of inaction honestly: missed sales, manual workload, reporting delay, compliance failure, weak reputation, customer loss, or opportunity cost.

## 3. Product And UX Standard

- Reduce effort before adding features. Premium users should feel guided, not overloaded.
- First value must arrive quickly: sample data, configured defaults, guided setup, a meaningful dashboard, or a completed first transaction.
- Every important screen needs loading, empty, error, success, permission, audit, export, and recovery states where relevant.
- Dashboards must answer executive questions: what changed, why it matters, what is at risk, who owns action, and what decision is required.
- Content, labels, and microcopy must communicate consequence, confidence, and next action.
- Visual design must feel intentional, restrained, accessible, fast, and consistent; do not rely on decorative luxury cues to hide weak product quality.

## 4. Financial, Control, And Reporting Depth

- Any system touching money must define accounts, ledgers or transaction records, approvals, audit trails, reconciliation, taxation/VAT where relevant, refunds, reversals, and role separation.
- POS and ERP tools must cover inventory, costing, margins, discounts, cash/card/mobile money, returns, permissions, daily close, exception reports, and management summaries.
- Billing/subscription tools must cover invoices, receipts, collections, arrears, revenue recognition implications, adjustments, and customer statements.
- Reports must be decision-grade: source, period, currency, assumptions, filters, export format, owner, and reconciliation path.
- Controls should reduce leakage without making honest work slow: maker-checker, limits, alerts, exception queues, audit logs, and month-end close support.

## 5. Proof And Packaging

- Package the product as a premium offer: name, promise, scope, demo, screenshots, onboarding, training, support, documentation, review cadence, and handover assets.
- Make service quality visible before purchase: process map, milestones, sample deliverables, implementation checklist, risk register, support path, and escalation rules.
- Build authority assets: diagnostic, benchmark, checklist, ROI calculator, comparison guide, implementation roadmap, case study, executive briefing, or teardown.
- For websites, content must support SEO, GEO, digital PR, conversion, trust, and lead qualification. The website is both the client's marketing asset and the agency's public proof.
- Claims require proof. If proof is not available, soften the claim and add the proof-building task to the scope.

## 6. Selling And Adoption

- Use discovery before pitching: situation, problem, implication, payoff, decision process, current alternatives, and risk.
- Outreach and proposals must show relevance: trigger event, business issue, role-specific pain, evidence, and a concrete next step.
- Demos should follow the buyer's workflow and business case, not a feature tour.
- Implementation must include adoption: kickoff, training, champion enablement, data migration, success metrics, support, review, and expansion plan.
- High-value customers need confidence after purchase: cadence, reporting, proactive issue handling, documented decisions, and renewal value review.

## Blocking Red Flags

- Premium price with commodity onboarding, weak reporting, poor controls, or vague support.
- Website or app looks polished but cannot explain value, rank, convert, measure, or prove trust.
- Pricing is presented before value, buyer fit, risk, and scope boundaries are clear.
- Executive buyers cannot see ROI, risk reduction, governance, or decision-ready reporting.
- The system competes only on cheapness against QuickBooks, Tally, spreadsheets, templates, or low-cost agencies.
