# Hiring Rubrics

Hire to a written success profile. Without one, you hire to vibes — and you will keep wondering why your best AEs' referrals underperform.

## Per-role success profiles

### SDR — success profile

| Behaviour | Evidence | Disqualifier |
|---|---|---|
| Activity discipline | Previous role with cold-call or heavy outbound KPI met consistently | Evasive about daily activity numbers |
| Resilience | Can describe a recent setback and what they did next | Blames previous employer for everything |
| Coachability | Asks clarifying questions; adjusts approach during the interview | Defensive when challenged |
| Written communication | Clean, specific, personalised outbound email on the spot | Generic, templated, or riddled with errors |
| Commercial curiosity | Reads about the buyer's business; asks about pipeline math | Does not know the job's numbers |

### AE — success profile

| Behaviour | Evidence | Disqualifier |
|---|---|---|
| Discovery rigour | Demonstrates MEDDIC/SPICED in a role-play | Demos before discovery |
| Storytelling | Connects features to quantified customer pain | Pitches features |
| Forecast calibration | Describes recent commits and accuracy | Cannot name their attainment or cycle time |
| Champion building | Describes a deal where they enabled a champion internally | Thinks "champion" is any friendly contact |
| Deal closure | Handles pricing, legal, and procurement without flinching | Never owned a close through procurement |
| Commercial maturity | Talks ACV, cycle time, win rate without prompting | Vague about numbers |

### CSM — success profile

| Behaviour | Evidence | Disqualifier |
|---|---|---|
| Adoption thinking | Describes how they drove product adoption, not just happiness | Equates "success" with "customer is nice to me" |
| Expansion | Has identified and closed expansion without AE holding their hand | Passive, waits for expansion to emerge |
| Hard conversations | Example of having told a customer "no" and kept the relationship | Conflict-avoidant |
| Data fluency | Uses product usage data to prioritise accounts | Managed by feelings |
| Renewal ownership | Has run a renewal (forecast, negotiation, paperwork) | Thinks renewal is "admin" |

## Interview loop

Standard 4-stage loop (AE role, adjust per role):

1. **Recruiter screen (30 min)**: motivation, comp expectations, basic fit. Gate: ICP match (motion experience, segment, ACV band).
2. **Hiring manager (45 min)**: behavioural interview against the success profile; probes into 2-3 recent deals.
3. **Role-play (60 min)**: structured selling simulation (see below). Observer + role-play partner.
4. **Panel (3x45 min)**: CRO (or skip-level), peer AE, cross-functional (marketing or CS).
5. **Reference calls (45 min each)**: 3 on-list + at least 1 back-channel.

Reject fast at any stage. "Nice but not a fit" is a real no.

## Role-play design

### For AEs

Scenario: "You are the AE at [our company]. The prospect is a VP Operations at a 400-person logistics firm evaluating alternatives to their current tool. The call is the first discovery call after an SDR booked the meeting."

Role-play partner plays the buyer with a brief: has pain, is busy, will not volunteer budget, has a competitor in mind, prefers not to involve IT.

Observer rubric (1-5 per dimension):

- Opening and agenda setting.
- Discovery depth (pain, impact, process, criteria).
- Active listening (ratio; reflection; summarising).
- Objection handling (specific: "We already have X").
- Next-step commitment.
- CRM-ready notes produced post-call (5-minute follow-up written during the interview).

Pass bar: average >=4.0, no dimension below 3.

### For SDRs

Scenario: live cold-call with a (simulated) prospect. 5 minutes to hook interest and book a meeting. Plus: write a personalised outbound email on the spot to a named target.

### For CSMs

Scenario: a customer who has stalled adoption and is signalling churn. 30-minute call to diagnose and propose a plan. Observe: listening, root-cause questions, willingness to push back.

```text
  4. ____________________________    Score 1-5: ___   Evidence: _________

  [ ] Blames previous employer
  [ ] Evasive on numbers
  [ ] Feature-pitched instead of discovered
  [ ] Over-claims success ("I closed the biggest deal in the company")
  [ ] Cannot describe a recent loss

```

Panel rule: any "strong no hire" from a trained interviewer is a veto.

## Reference calls

On-list references (chosen by the candidate) and back-channel references (chosen by you) cover different ground.

### On-list questions

- How would you describe their coachability?
- What is one thing they need to work on?
- Would you hire them again? (Silence or hedging is a no.)
- What is their attainment percentile on your team?

### Back-channel questions

- Can you describe a deal where they shone, and one where they struggled?
- How did they handle coaching or feedback?
- Would they thrive in [your stage: early, scaling, mature]?

If the candidate refuses to provide references from their most recent manager, it is almost always a no.

## Common red flags

- **Inflated numbers**: "I closed $4M last year" in a role where team total was $3M. Ask for disaggregation.
- **Pattern of short tenures**: multiple <12 month stays without structural explanation (acquisition, layoff).
- **No losses**: every rep has lost; a candidate who cannot name one is not reflective.
- **Product infatuation without commercial story**: loves the product, cannot describe unit economics.
- **Vague on comp**: fuzzy about their own OTE, attainment, or pay history.
- **Cannot explain territory or book**: does not know where their pipeline came from.

## Diversity and fairness

- Use the same rubric and scorecard for every candidate in a role.
- Panel composition includes at least one interviewer outside sales (marketing, CS, finance) to reduce groupthink.
- Structured questions (not "tell me about yourself") reduce bias.
- Record rubric scores before discussion to avoid anchoring.

## Anti-patterns

- Hiring to fill a seat by end-of-quarter — you will pay for the bad hire for a year.
- Letting the loudest interviewer set the decision — rubrics and written scores first, discussion second.
- Skipping back-channel references for "senior" candidates — seniority is precisely where bad hires cost the most.
- Hiring friends of founders without the loop — erodes the standard for the whole team.
- Role-play is optional — if you skip it, you are hiring on narrative.
